Abstract

This paper examines the core technologies that enable airborne stealth and their role in reducing aircraft detectability across radar and infrared spectrums. Originating with the F-117A in 1977, stealth design has advanced through innovations in aircraft shaping, radar-absorbing materials (RAM), active cancellation methods, and infrared suppression techniques. Each element contributes to minimizing the radar cross section and thermal signature by absorbing or scattering signals. Emerging materials such as carbon-based composites and advanced coatings have further improved low observability and operational effectiveness. Although complete invisibility remains unachievable, the integration of these systems provides modern aircraft with a significant tactical advantage by delaying or preventing detection in complex threat environments.

Introduction

Stealth technology was first developed and used in 1977 where Lockheed Martin’s Skunks Work division was able to create an aircraft which was virtually undetectable to radars; this being the F-117A. The most notable stealth aircraft is the B-2 Bomber which was first introduced in 1988 and is still being used for surveillance and other uses to this day. The components of an aircraft which can cause it to fly under the radar are as follows: The shaping of the aircraft’s chassis, radar absorbing materials, active Cancellation & infared Stealth.

Shaping of the aircrafts chassis

The main variable which is to be minimised by the shaping is the radar cross section, this being the area of the aircraft visible to the radar. The radar cross section (RCS) is the measure of a target's ability to reflect radar signals in the direction of the radar receiver(Nicolaescu and Oroian, 2001). One way this can be utilized is by having smooth surfaces, as if cavities in the chassis were present the radar is able to detect it via the diffraction of the radio waves; other major components which affect the radar cross section would be the cockpit but as in the F-117A and the B2-Bomber a low-profile canopy is used; the canopy windows are sloped to scatter the radio waves away from their direction (Aronstein and Piccirillo, 1997). It is one of the most important features as the B2 Spirit has a wingspan of 52.4m but with the RAM its RCS is less than 0.1m.

Radar absorbing material (RAM)

Radar absorbing material are used to reduce the RCS, causing it to appear smaller on a radar. This material contains properties which absorb and conduct incident radar waves. A way of which this material is applied to the aircraft is via bonding or a spray-painting process (Jha and Aswale, 2016). It was first developed by Lockheed Martin and used in the A-12 and SR-71 (Dranidis, 2003). RAM works by converting the electromagnetic energy from the radar into heat energy, this is done via electric and magnetic losses within the material. It is designed to work at specific frequencies, therefore multiple layers of RAM are used to cover a wider range of frequencies (Jha and Aswale, 2016).It is one of the most important features as the B2 Spirit has a wingspan of 52.4m but with the RAM its RCS is less than 0.1m.

Active Cancellation System

Active Cancellation is a method used to produce an artificial radiation field, containing the same amplitude and frequency but is in opposite phase from the radar's target field; leading to the radar receiving an identical field without the trace of the target (aircraft), (Xu and Xu, 2014). This method is still in the research phase and has not been implemented into any aircraft to date. The main issue with this method is the time delay between the radar signal being received and the cancellation signal being transmitted; if this delay is too long then the radar will be able to detect the aircraft. (Xu and Xu, 2014).

Infrared Stealth

All objects known to man which release heat produce thermal radiation. This thermal radiation causes the atoms and electrons in the world to get excited and ionised which leads to the creation of electromagnetic waves. The electromagnetic waves are made up of visible light, infrared radiation and microwaves. Infrared radiation have a wavelength of 0.76-1000 μm. Infrared sensors and cameras can be used to see objects which may be invisible to the naked eye. To prevent an aircraft from being spotted they are coated in a 'Infrared stealth coating' (Zhou et al., 2024), which is made of a carbon-based conductive film on a foam like foundation which provides conductivity which allows for the absorption of multiple of infrared waves at different frequencies(Liu, Zhao and Han, 2025). By reducing the emissivity of an aircraft, the surface temperature needs to also be regulated to achieve 'infrared stealth', (Kang et al., 2024) this is due to Boltzmann's law which tells us that "the relationship between the emitted power P of the thermal radiation of a blackbody object and the object's temperature T is P=CTᵃ where C is a constant."  (Wellons, 2007). Therefore by reducing the temperature of the aircraft the emitted power is also reduced leading to a lower infrared signature.

Conclusion

Advances in stealth technology within aircraft have revolutionized modern military aviation by minimizing the detection capabilities of aircraft over electromagnetic and infrared spectra. The effectiveness of stealth depends on the integration of low-observable shaping, radar-absorbing materials, infrared signature suppression, and active cancellation methods of more recent development. All these domains operate to absorb, deflect, or neutralize detectable emissions. Although perfect invisibility is still theoretically impossible, stealth performance continues to improve through advances in materials science and signal-processing technologies. Stealth aircraft will undoubtedly remain a critical component of modern defense capability, continuously evolving as tracking and sensing systems grow increasingly sophisticated.
